Anime Basics: Where to Begin

The first thing to understand when getting into anime is how genres work within Japanese animation. Much like in Western media, anime genres are about marketing and target audiences rather than strict rules.

Anime is generally divided into four main demographic categories: Shōnen, Shōjo, Seinen, and Josei.

These labels don't limit who can enjoy a series—they simply describe who it was originally made for.

Shōnen

Action-packed stories about growth, friendship, and adventure.

Naruto, One Piece, My Hero Academia

Shōjo

Emotion-driven stories focusing on relationships and self-discovery.

Sailor Moon, Fruits Basket

Seinen

Mature themes with complex narratives and darker tones.

Attack on Titan, Cowboy Bebop

Josei

Realistic portrayals of adult life, romance, and personal struggles.

Nana, Paradise Kiss

A Brief History of Anime

Anime has its roots in early 20th-century Japan, with some of the first animated films appearing in the 1910s like The Dull Sword which is pictured above. Influenced by Western animation, Japanese animators gradually developed a unique visual style and approach to storytelling.

After World War II, anime grew alongside television. Osamu Tezuka's Astro Boy (1963) helped define many of anime's visual conventions. By the 1970s and 1980s, anime expanded into new genres and began targeting audiences beyond children.

In the 1990s and early 2000s, anime reached global audiences through television and home video. Today, anime is a worldwide cultural phenomenon, made widely accessible through streaming platforms and enjoyed by people of all ages.
